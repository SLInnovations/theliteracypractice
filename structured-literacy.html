What It Is

Instruction built on how the brain actually learns to read.

The research has been building for decades. The most effective reading and writing instruction is explicit, systematic, and cumulative, skills are taught directly, in a logical sequence, with each new concept building on what came before.

This is different from the way most of us were taught. Traditional classrooms often rely on exposure and immersion, reading a lot, seeing words repeatedly, hoping things click. For most students, that works fine. For students with dyslexia, ADHD, or processing differences, it doesn't. And for plenty of students without any formal diagnosis, it still leaves gaps.

Explicit, systematic instruction doesn't assume the brain will figure it out. It teaches the code, directly, step by step, with deliberate practice built in at every stage.

What "systematic" actually means

Systematic means there's a sequence and it's intentional. We don't jump to paragraphs before sentences are solid. We don't introduce a complex spelling pattern before simpler ones are automatic. The sequence exists because of how reading and spelling work in the brain, and following it matters.

Every lesson I teach is designed around where your child actually is in that sequence. The assessment tells me where to begin. The roadmap tells me where we're going. The instruction gets us there.

Why writing sometimes gets left out

Most of the conversation about reading science focuses on reading. Writing gets much less attention, even though the same principles apply. Explicit instruction in sentence structure, text organization, and the writing process produces results. Students don't discover how to write, they learn how to write. Systematically. From one sentence.

In plain language:

How I Teach

The principles behind every lesson.

01

Explicit

Skills are taught directly. Nothing is implied, assumed, or left to discovery. If a student needs to know it, I teach it out loud.

02

Systematic

There's a sequence and it's followed intentionally. Each skill builds on the one before. No gaps, no skipping ahead.

03

Cumulative

Every new skill builds on the ones before it. Old skills don't disappear. They get reviewed, applied, and reinforced until they're automatic, not just remembered.

04

Diagnostic

Teaching starts where the student actually is. Assessment tells me where to begin. Progress data tells me when to move forward.

05

Multisensory

Visual, auditory, and kinesthetic channels work together. Students see it, say it, and feel it. More pathways mean more retrieval.

06

Responsive

The plan adjusts. If something isn't working, I change the approach.

Common Misconceptions

Things I hear a lot.

Myth

"They'll catch up on their own."

Reading gaps don't typically close without instruction. The research is clear on this: students who fall behind in early reading tend to stay behind without targeted intervention.

Reality

Gaps need to be filled directly.

The good news: with the right instruction, students at any point can build the skills they're missing. It's not too late at any age.

Myth

"More reading practice will fix it."

Reading more helps students who already have solid reading skills. For students with gaps, more exposure to text they can't fully decode reinforces bad habits.

Reality

The skill has to come first.

Once the foundational skills are solid, reading widely and often becomes one of the best things a child can do. The foundation has to come first.
